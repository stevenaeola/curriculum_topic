Course Aims
Produce graduates with the skills needed to secure employment and embark upon a professional career within computer science or software development.
• Enable students to explore the complex body of knowledge associated with computer science and software development.
• Develop the students’ cognitive skills essential for analysing, designing and evaluating software and computer-based systems.
• Equip students with the industry-relevant knowledge and skills required to construct software and computer-based systems.
• Produce graduates who will uphold the legal, ethical and professional standards expected of a computer scientist or software developer, whilst appreciating the social, economic and environmental impact of computing on the world.
• Encourage students to seek professional employment or pursue further study.

Learning & Teaching Methods
The learning and teaching methods employed within the course reflect the aims and key priority areas of the Learning & Teaching Strategy. A variety of learning and teaching methods are employed throughout the course to provide students with the skills, knowledge and tools to thrive and achieve sustainable success within the global workplace, through becoming research active, future ready, globally connected, socially and ethically engaged and digitally empowered.

Lectures, including presentations from guest speakers, are used to deliver relevant subject specific content. Subjects are explored in-depth in tutor-led tutorials and student-led seminars, and contributions to forum discussions further develop students’ cognitive/intellectual skills and understanding of the subject material.

Modules incorporate significant practical implementation of taught material. Students’ practical skills are developed through practical tutorials and laboratory sessions, problem-based learning and self-study material in key level 4, 5 and 6 modules, to their working on industrially relevant, real-world scenarios where practical.

Keynote lectures are used to introduce topics that are supported through independent study materials and further developed by module tutors with class-based activities.

Learning experiences are enhanced through use of industry standard hardware and software, enabling classroom learning to complement workplace practices. Students will have access to specialist laboratories for technical modules with long opening hours to help them learn flexibly. This includes dedicated computing rooms (e.g. programming and networking labs).

Development of transferable skills and lifelong learning are core themes throughout the course. The management of own learning and self-development is addressed primarily in the completion of in-course assessments. PDP is promoted and actively supported at every stage (e.g. through individual or developmental reflection, team work and production of professional documentation/presentations).

An essential characteristic of a computing professional is the awareness and understanding of the legal, social, ethical and professional issues (LSEPIs) relevant to their subject area. The consideration and assessment of student understanding of LSEPIs is embedded in appropriate core modules at levels 4, 5 and 6.

Peer review is facilitated through a number of opportunities, such as critique sessions, team working sessions and student led formative feedback sessions.

The course encourages students to be considerate of their working environment and stakeholders including any resultant impact from systems analysis, development, implementation and testing.

Some modules adopt a “Flipped Classroom” approach to learning, supported via the University’s Virtual Learning Environment (VLE) and other tools such as Panopto ReView. All modules have an online presence via the VLE, enabling students to continue to engage with the learning resources and work collaboratively, both with peers and tutors through discussions and group areas, outside of timetabled sessions. Each module site on the VLE also has links to electronic library resources and services. The project modules will utilise synchronous sessions with regular academic meetings to check on progress and offer formative feedback.

In addition, a number of modules encourage participation within online developer communities.

Throughout the course, there is a strong commitment to academic excellence which involves students in discipline-based research. Students will develop their research and inquiry skills and techniques; learn about current and emerging techniques and trends in the discipline; engage in discussions and undertake their own research and inquiry.

To complement the skills taught within the school, Academic Librarians run workshops on a range of academic topics (such as reflective writing and referencing). They also offer one-to-one support sessions for students.

Outside of timetabled sessions, students are required to continue their exploration of the subject knowledge and skills development. IT laboratories and studios are available for freelance study and provide access to specialist hardware and software, in addition to use of AppsAnywhere for remote connections.

The course will also include guest speakers, seminars and workshops on current research and emerging topics. These will include inquiry-based learning and activities that mirror research and/or industry focused processes in the discipline. Additionally, they provide students with a bibliography of important and recent academic and professional research publications in the discipline.

The School works with the Learning Hub in Student and Library Services to offer a holistic and inclusive service for all students. The learning advisors and academic librarians offer a range of support to students to help them achieve their potential in coursework, particularly with study skills and writing. A range of approaches for learning are offered including drop-in, one-to-one tutorials and workshops including the succeed@tees workshops.

Assessment and Feedback
The assessment strategy is based on the University’s Learning and Teaching Strategic Plan (LTSP) and employs a range of assessment methods. During assessments, students are provided with summative and formative future-facing feedback to support their development.

Students are primarily assessed through in-course assessments, although use is made of time-constrained tests where appropriate. Wherever possible in-course assessments will be industry-related or have a business or commercial context. In modules that involve external clients, students receive pertinent feedback from these clients at identified stages of a project. Specific marking rubrics or feedback methods are designed for individual modules to enable each student to receive constructive individual feedback.

Assessment and feedback processes are described in Module Guides and Assessment Specifications, ensuring constructive alignment with module and course learning outcomes. Assessments are designed to be inclusive, regardless of student demographics and all module assessments are moderated and marked via formal school processes. Assessment weightings and timing take into consideration all module assessments for the course to reduce the risk of over-assessment points.

Project modules provide an opportunity to demonstrate transferable skills due to the extent of development work and the requirement to work in teams, document solutions and orally defend decisions. Where peer assessment is utilised, it is moderated by staff. Group work is also used in non-project modules to incorporate significant transferable skills development. The final year project module is a large scale, individual piece of work, involving the production of a substantial artefact with a video walkthrough and culminating in the writing of a report. The project gives the student an opportunity to further develop their specialism in a specific area.

Formative assessment and feedback sessions are scheduled within modules (typically from week five onwards) to allow students to identify areas for improvement and continue good practice. Lab-based sessions provide an opportunity for students to discuss and demonstrate their progress and for the module teaching team to provide on-going verbal feedback.

Where students are required to complete re-assessment they will be notified via e-vision and supported through the VLE. Students will be guided to read their feedback to identify areas for improvement and to raise any queries with the module leader.

Please note that option modules within this Course Specification are ‘indicative’ and may be subject to change. A current list of option modules can be provided on request.

Module Descriptor
Cognitive technologies, such as Artificial intelligence (AI), are increasingly being used to solve business problems. Artificial intelligence, or AI, is concerned with not just understanding but also building intelligent entities, machines that can compute how to act effectively and safely in a wide variety of novel situations. 

This module provides students with a general introduction to artificial intelligence, discussing real-world applications of AI, and its transformative power in our daily life. Thus, this module includes the fundamental concepts of AI, common frameworks used in the analysis and design of intelligent systems, generic algorithms used for their implementation and major techniques used in problem solving. The target is to provide students with the knowledge that will allow you to understand how to address AI problems, studying a wide range of AI techniques, applications, and developing solutions. 

This module also introduces popular applications of AI (for example, game design, virtual agents, robotics etc.) and benefits of using AI (for example, how to enhance efficiency, productivity, and reduce costs). 

The module will be assessed by a single in course assessment that will require the evaluation of the use of AI in recent and well-known products, and the development of a framework solution using AI techniques.

Learning Strategy
The learning strategy of this module is focussed on preparing students for sustained success within the global workplace, through becoming research active, future ready, globally connected, socially and ethically engaged, and digitally empowered.

Students will be introduced to theoretical concepts via lectures and laboratory sessions will develop the students' understanding of these concepts through problem-based learning. A significant proportion of the learning in this module will be student centred.

Lecture presentations will be reinforced with a task-based approach to learning, where students apply and explore the topics presented in the lecture. The techniques will be explored partly through students’ own implementations but also by utilising AI techniques and algorithms. Students will use the lab sessions to explore real-world applications of AI, explore frameworks and algorithms and implement an AI solution by reasoning and applying AI techniques to a single or a small collection of real-world case studies chosen by student. Individual support and feedback will be provided in weekly practical sessions.

The module will embrace active learning principles to engage students throughout their studies, using a problem or enquiry-based approach. Emphasis will be on student– centred activity which incrementally builds knowledge and skill through AfL (Assessment for Learning) exercises and related module learning activities.

The module has been designed in accordance with Teesside University’s Future Facing Learning (FFL) Strategy: a distinct pedagogic approach underpinning our commitment to delivering Education 4.0. The University Academic Enhancement Framework (AEF) provides the structure through which FFL is embedded with academic practice and the module has drawn upon the AEF to inform its design. For example, ensuring students are
digitally empowered and future ready through use of common industry standard digital tools to support learning and assessment.

The module will be supported by a Virtual Learning Environment (VLE). The VLE will be used to provide a range of materials to inform and support learning to suit a variety of learning styles and will form the basis of electronic communication with students in relation to their modules. It will also be used to facilitate electronic submission and feedback of summative assessment work.

To deliver a learner-centred student experience as defined in the LTSP and the Academic Enhancement Framework (AEF), in particular the Student Success, Student Voice, Transitions and Wellbeing themes. Induction

Contact with new students is established prior to induction week. Students are sent information about their modules, pathway structure and the support mechanisms available within the School.

Student engagement begins in the weeks leading up to induction week with access to the University VLE and the Student Intranet. Special arrangements are made for international students who are given access to the VLE on arrival. International students are provided with additional English language support through central services.

Induction week comprises a series of events to impart the aims of the course, familiarise students with facilities and mechanisms and integrate students socially. A full programme of events and information sessions is arranged to make the student feel welcome, ensure they are aware of what is expected of them and to promote a cohort identity. Some activities that are normally included are:

• Course Leader Welcome including introductions to the teaching team and course structure.
• IT and Digital Services talk and Lab Session including an introduction to key IT systems
• Student and Library Services introduction
• Problem based learning activities
• Key Lectures on modules
• Advice and guidance on support arrangements and student regulations.
• Social activities to encourage the development of cohort identity and sense of belonging

The academic school also incorporates extended induction activities, with topics such as library skills, ethics and referencing and employability skills spread through the year.

Distinctive Features
There is stronger emphasis on the industry-relevant, practical treatment of topics compared with many other Computer Science courses in the UK, producing graduates with expertise of contemporary professional practice.

The study of a real-world problem in level 5 gives the student valuable experience working on a substantial, collaborative project. Working in small teams, they will prepare a product that satisfies a realistic brief, then 'pitch' that product to a panel of academics and industry experts.

Assessment favours industry-relevant, practical in-course assignments over traditional examinations to provide students with a real-world experience of solving computer science and software development problems.

Teaching staff represent a balanced mix of internationally-recognised research expertise and up-to-date practical industry experience.

The development of professional transferable skills and personal development planning are strong themes that run throughout the programme.

Students have the opportunity to undertake a year-long supervised work placement between the second and third year, providing an opportunity to develop employment prospects by providing a real-world experience which will develop new skills and deeper understanding of the subject.

The programme is accredited by the Chartered Institute for IT (BCS) and graduates may obtain full or partial exemption from BCS exams.

Students will have access to specialised laboratory hardware and software throughout the course, ensuring that their learning experience prepares them for working in industry. Our continued employer collaboration enables us to respond to industry need and provide repeated exposure points to prospective employers.

The Expo series is a long-standing series of student showcase events developed by the School offering all students the opportunity to showcase their work to invited industry partners. At ExpoTees (final year) students can compete for various internships and competitions which are on offer. In addition, first and second year students will have the opportunity to engage in the ExpoTees sister events, ExpoTential and ExpoTalent. ExpoTential (for first year students) and ExpoTalent (for second year students) provide a platform to practice for ExpoTees and develop presentation skills. ExpoTalent is also designed as a great opportunity for students to meet businesses and potentially secure work placements and internships.
