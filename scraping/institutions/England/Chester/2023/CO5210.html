As this module incorporates assessed group work, evidence of at least 75% of individual engagement with the team and process is required. Tutors monitor live attendance via online collaborative tools utilised in the module for each scheduled session. This will be made clear to all students at the start of the module. 

If the engagement requirement is not met by any individual student, the mark for that person will be capped at 40%.

It is the responsibility of individual students to make sure their engagement is sufficient. Due to the importance of setting out clear roles and responsibilities from the outset, any negative influence of one individual will not affect other team members' potential to achieve.

  • Applying best practice in the design of user interfaces for computer games and "mainstream" computer systems.
  • Further study of cognition theory, human factors; their relevance to interaction & "usability" and overall user experience design.
  • Practical design and prototyping of meaningful user interfaces for computer games and mainstream computer systems.
  • Conducting iterative team-based design activities using recommended industry-approved frameworks.
  • Learning the fundamental differences (and similarities) between game UX and mainstream UX.
  • Introduction to careers in game UX.

  • To encourage the effective application of advanced player-centred design techniques.
  • To promote effective information architecture for game menus and HUDs.
  • To show how applying contemporary visual design principles and inclusivity guidelines can enhance the user experience for players.
  • To provide opportunities to participate in team-based problem-solving activities for producing design solutions in games.
  • To provide opportunities to plan and moderate usability / play testing and evaluation of games.

  • Tutor directed and self directed study.
  • Lectures, workshops/tutorials and seminars.
  • Online learning as directed, plus demonstrations & practical exercises.
  • Formative feedback will be given during contact time, by email and via virtual learning environments where appropriate.

  1. Demonstrate an appreciation of the key differences between 'mainstream' and game UX.
  2. Demonstrate a critical understanding of expert game UX theory (including Hodent's (2017) seven pillars through the evaluation of commercial games.
  3. Demonstrate the ability to work collaboratively (and efficiently) towards understanding and solving a design problem.
  4. Produce player-centred design solutions as part of the creation of a game user interface prototype.
  5. Demonstrate an awareness of accessibility issues in games and interactive mainstream systems.
